Introduction: The Low-Resource Curation Paradigm
The trajectory of large language model (LLM) research has increasingly prioritized data
efficiency and targeted optimization over raw parameter scaling.1 As models approach the
asymptotic limits of available human-generated text, the focus has shifted toward self-curated
dataset optimization, wherein models actively filter, refine, and select their own training data to
maximize supervised fine-tuning (SFT) efficacy.2 This paradigm is particularly critical for
lightweight architectures, where parameter capacity acts as a strict bottleneck. In such
constrained environments, exposing a model to overly complex, noisy, or dense data does not
simply result in inefficient learning; it actively degrades alignment and induces catastrophic
forgetting by exceeding the model's representational capacity.4 Consequently, the ability to
accurately assess and select training data based on its intrinsic cognitive difficulty has emerged
as a paramount challenge in contemporary machine learning.2
This report details an exhaustive theoretical, mathematical, and experimental framework for
investigating self-curated dataset optimization through the precise lens of token-masking
mechanics. The focal point of this investigation utilizes the Qwen-2.5-0.5B model, an advanced
lightweight architecture specifically chosen for its low hardware requirements, rapid iteration
capabilities, and robust baseline performance across diverse cognitive tasks.5 By establishing
two independent data tracks—one dedicated to semantic understanding and linguistic nuance,
and the other rigorously focused on logical and mathematical reasoning—the research isolates
the impact of specialized data curation on distinct cognitive modalities.7
The core theoretical novelty of this framework lies in the comparative analysis of two distinct
token-masking strategies used to assign a "difficulty score" to candidate training samples
dynamically. Strategy A, conceptualized as Input Masking, obscures key informational tokens
within the prompt context to evaluate the model's generation stability and its reliance on
specific instructional cues. Strategy B, defined as Output Masking, obscures target tokens
within the historical answer sequence to evaluate the model's predictive certainty and internal
logical consistency. By defining a mathematically rigorous yet computationally lightweight
framework for these difficulty scores, the research enables the selective curation of
fine-tuning subsets that perfectly match the model's optimal learning threshold.4
This comprehensive proposal encompasses a deep literature review to contextualize the
prevailing research gap, formal mathematical definitions for the proposed difficulty metrics
grounded in information theory, a highly detailed experimental protocol tailored for single-GPU
consumer environments, a robust multi-dimensional evaluation matrix, and a strategic

framework designed to ensure the academic validity of the findings irrespective of the
empirical outcomes.

The foundation of this research rests upon three intersecting domains of machine learning:
self-distillation and automated data selection, curriculum learning guided by perplexity and
difficulty metrics, and token-level masking mechanisms. Analyzing the current state of these
fields reveals critical insights into how lightweight models process information, highlighting the
specific research gap that the proposed input versus output masking paradigm addresses.

Dataset Pruning and Curriculum Learning via Difficulty Scoring
The long-standing heuristic that expanding dataset size universally improves model capability
has been systematically dismantled in the context of supervised fine-tuning. Recent empirical
phenomena and theoretical analyses demonstrate that effective data selection must adapt
dynamically to both the available data scale and the specific architectural capability of the
target model.2 A pivotal finding in recent literature is that preference data varies significantly in
cognitive difficulty, and exposing a model to overly difficult examples actively hinders alignment
by exceeding the model's parameter capacity.4 This underscores a critical relationship between
data selection and model scale: a 0.5-billion parameter model simply cannot process the same
complexity of training data as a 70-billion parameter model without suffering representational
collapse.
To operationalize the concept of "difficulty," researchers have historically relied on
perplexity-based filtering methodologies. A large perplexity score typically indicates that a data
sample is difficult, rare, or anomalous relative to the model's pre-trained distribution.8 Early
implementations utilized fixed n-gram language models to calculate perplexity across
candidate datasets, typically selecting examples with intermediate values to form optimal
training curriculums.2 As LLMs evolved, these metrics became self-referential. Advancements
in this space introduced the Instruction-Following Difficulty (IFD) metric, a self-guided method
that quantifies the challenge of responding to a specific instruction without relying on external
evaluator models or human annotation.9
The IFD score is mathematically defined as the ratio of the perplexity of the response
conditioned on the instruction to the unconditional perplexity of the response.11 A lower IFD
score indicates that the instruction significantly aided the model in generating the response,
whereas a higher score suggests a semantic disconnect or high inherent difficulty.11 Further
enhancements to this concept, such as the Token-Selective Hierarchical Data Selection
(T-SHIRT) framework, identified critical limitations in sample-level evaluation.12 T-SHIRT
introduced the Selective-IFD (S-IFD) score, which isolates token-level informativeness, thereby
preventing the erroneous selection of samples based on superficial lexical features rather than
true semantic or structural quality.12
Furthermore, research into Adaptive Difficulty in reinforcement learning has demonstrated that

prioritizing questions of moderate difficulty yields the most informative learning signals,
drastically reducing fine-tuning time while maintaining state-of-the-art performance
benchmarks.1 This principle of targeting the "median difficulty bracket" forms the theoretical
justification for the data filtering mechanisms proposed in this experimental framework.

Token-Masking Mechanisms and Forgetting Mitigation
Token-masking has traditionally been associated with pre-training objectives, most notably
Masked Language Modeling (MLM) utilized in BERT architectures, as well as visual input
masking tasks like Masked Region Modeling (MRM).15 However, recent literature has
repurposed masking as a highly effective tool for data curation, dataset pruning, and the active
mitigation of catastrophic forgetting during the fine-tuning phase.
The introduction of Selective Token Masking (STM) represents a significant advancement in this
specific domain. STM operates on the principle that fine-tuning an LLM on high-perplexity
tokens correlates strongly with the degradation of a model's general, out-of-domain
capabilities.17 By actively filtering out or masking these high-perplexity tokens during the
supervised fine-tuning stage, STM blocks backpropagation from these unstable elements,
thereby enabling what researchers term "low-perplexity training".18 Empirical evidence robustly
demonstrates that STM not only improves target task performance but virtually eliminates the
backward transfer of catastrophic forgetting, achieving capabilities comparable to models
trained on highly curated, computationally expensive self-generated data.20
Moreover, alternative masking mechanisms have been explored to enhance pure reasoning
capabilities. For instance, "retrieved token masking" prevents unintended learning dynamics by
excluding specific contextual elements from the reinforcement learning loss computation,
yielding substantial improvements in complex question-answering benchmarks.21 Other
innovative approaches, such as DiffuMask, utilize diffusion language models to predict token
retention masks entirely in parallel, enabling efficient and scalable prompt compression by
isolating hierarchical pruning behaviors.22

The Research Gap: Differentiated Masking for Diagnostic Curation
While the existing literature heavily utilizes perplexity and token masking, it predominantly
applies these concepts in a uniform, reactive, or protective manner. Methods like STM mask
high-perplexity tokens in the target sequence specifically to protect the model's weights
during backpropagation.19 Conversely, difficulty metrics like IFD compare full prompt
conditioning against zero prompt conditioning to generate a passive difficulty score.11
The specific, highly novel research gap this thesis fills is the active, differentiated use of
selective token masking (Input vs. Output) as an upfront diagnostic tool to generate nuanced
difficulty scores for data curation, rather than deploying it merely as a protective training
mechanism.
Currently, no established framework systematically compares how input masking (perturbing
the prompt to test generation stability) versus output masking (perturbing the target to test
prediction certainty) differs in identifying the optimal curriculum for a lightweight model.
Strategy A hypothesizes that semantic understanding is highly sensitive to input context; thus,

input masking will effectively isolate samples that require robust reasoning over fragile
contextual cues. Strategy B hypothesizes that logical and mathematical reasoning relies on
strict sequential certainty; thus, output masking will effectively isolate samples where the
model's internal step-by-step derivation is unstable. By bridging token-masking mechanics
with difficulty-based dataset pruning, this research introduces a highly distinct,
computationally efficient paradigm for self-curated SFT alignment.

Experimental and Architectural Design
The transition from mathematical theory to empirical execution requires a meticulously defined
experimental protocol. This section details the architectural specifics of the chosen baseline
model, the curation of the source datasets, the procedural implementation of the masking

protocols, and the highly optimized fine-tuning recipe required to execute this methodology
under stringent hardware constraints.

1. Baseline Architecture: Qwen-2.5-0.5B Specifications
The selection of Qwen-2.5-0.5B as the baseline model is central to the thesis premise. With
0.49 billion total parameters (and 0.36 billion non-embedding parameters), the model is small
enough to train locally on consumer hardware while possessing an architecture sophisticated
enough to yield measurable performance variances under masking conditions.6
The model architecture utilizes a standard Transformer configuration with RoPE (Rotary
Positional Embeddings), SwiGLU activation functions, and RMSNorm for deep stabilization.6 It is
constructed with 24 hidden layers, a hidden dimension size of 896, and employs Grouped
Query Attention (GQA) with 14 query heads and 2 key-value heads.6 Notably, it supports a full
context length of 32,768 tokens and demonstrates proficiency in both instruction following and
structured data processing natively.23
Because the parameter size is highly constrained, its representational capacity acts as a
magnifying glass for dataset quality. Suboptimal, noisy, or overly complex data will quickly
induce catastrophic forgetting of its pre-trained linguistic priors.17 Therefore, the differences
between the datasets curated via Strategy A and Strategy B will be empirically pronounced and
highly visible during evaluation.

2. Data Tracks and Open-Source Selection
To test the hypothesis that input masking optimizes semantic curation while output masking
optimizes logical curation, the experiment requires two distinct, high-quality base datasets.
These datasets must be clean, open-source, and appropriately sized for a 0.5B model to
prevent prolonged processing bottlenecks.

3. Token Masking Protocol Implementation
The precise definition of what constitutes a masked token is critical to the integrity of the
experiment. Random masking introduces unacceptable statistical noise; therefore, a
deterministic, information-theoretic approach must be employed to guarantee reproducibility.
Protocol A: Input Token Masking Formulation
To mask the input , we employ a localized, entropy-based selection criteria combined with
basic Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging. Using a lightweight external library (e.g., SpaCy), we
identify Nouns, Verbs, and Adjectives within the prompt context. We then selectively mask
exactly 20% of these high-information tokens by replacing them with a distinct <|mask|> token.
For the mathematical track, numerical values and distinct operational keywords (e.g.,
"calculate", "derive") are prioritized for masking. This directly targets the structural integrity of
the prompt's instructions.
Protocol B: Output Token Masking Formulation
To mask the target output
, we utilize an iterative block-masking protocol. Because
autoregressive models rely heavily on immediate historical context, masking isolated tokens is
insufficient to disrupt sequence certainty. Instead, we mask sequential blocks (e.g., contiguous
spans of 3-5 tokens) at calculated intervals across the target sequence during the evaluation
forward pass. This forces the model to bridge larger contextual gaps, accurately reflecting the

structural stability of its internal reasoning chains.

4. Fine-Tuning Recipe for Single Consumer GPU
To guarantee the reproducibility of this methodology on a single consumer GPU (e.g., an
NVIDIA RTX 3060 Ti with 8GB VRAM or a single A100 slice) 27, the fine-tuning framework strictly
relies on Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) paired with 4-bit quantization (QLoRA).28
The integration of 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4) quantization significantly reduces the memory
footprint, enabling the training of the 0.5B model within a tight 4GB VRAM constraint, allowing
the remaining VRAM to process larger batch sizes and extensive context windows efficiently.27
The empirical parameters for the LoRA configuration are carefully calibrated to ensure that the
parameter updates are expressive enough to learn the curated datasets without overfitting.26

The evaluation matrix is fundamentally designed to definitively answer the core research
question: Does input masking versus output masking yield measurably different alignment
characteristics when applied to semantic versus logical tasks? Furthermore, the evaluation
must meticulously quantify the extent to which each curation strategy induces or mitigates
catastrophic forgetting of the model's pre-trained capabilities.

1. Benchmarking Semantic vs. Logical Alignment
To evaluate the fine-tuned models effectively, a diverse suite of standardized, zero-shot
benchmarks will be employed. These benchmarks are purposefully selected to independently
isolate semantic comprehension from strict deductive reasoning.
Semantic and Linguistic Assessment Benchmarks:
●​ ARC-Challenge (ARC-C): Evaluates the model's ability to answer complex, grade-school
science questions requiring robust semantic comprehension and conceptual synthesis.31
●​ HellaSwag (HS): Tests the model's capacity to perform commonsense natural language
inference and sentence completion. Success here strongly indicates that the model has
successfully retained broad linguistic priors.31
●​ MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding): Provides a comprehensive
overview of the model's general knowledge retention and instruction-following capability
across highly diverse subjects.31
Logical and Mathematical Assessment Benchmarks:
●​ GSM8K Test Set: Evaluates the model's deterministic, multi-step arithmetic reasoning.
The accuracy of the final generated answer depends entirely on the stability of the
intermediate logical steps.7
●​ MATH Dataset: A significantly more rigorous assessment of mathematical
problem-solving, requiring advanced algebraic and geometric derivation, testing the
absolute limits of the 0.5B parameter capacity.7
The comparative analysis will scrutinize the differential performance matrix resulting from
these evaluations. If the central hypothesis holds true, the Input-Masked Model should
demonstrate superior performance on ARC-C and HellaSwag, as its dataset was optimized for
stable contextual mapping. Conversely, the Output-Masked Model should demonstrate
superior performance on GSM8K and MATH, as its dataset was specifically optimized to
enforce internal sequential rigor.

2. Post-Tuning Robustness and Forgetting Metrics
In addition to evaluating pure performance benchmarks, the framework must quantify the
overall architectural stability of the models utilizing the Task Improvement (TI) and Backward
Transfer (BWT) metrics established heavily in the Selective Token Masking literature.18
Catastrophic forgetting remains a primary, persistent risk in supervised fine-tuning, especially
in models with less than one billion parameters.17 If the proposed difficulty curation strategies
successfully isolate the optimal learning threshold without exceeding capacity, the resulting
models should exhibit a highly positive Task Improvement on their target domains while
maintaining a near-zero or minimal negative Backward Transfer on non-target domains.20
By calculating the baseline perplexity of the fine-tuned models on a held-out corpus of raw
text, the evaluation will accurately determine if the selective masking strategies inadvertently
curated data distributions that warped the model's foundational linguistic geometry. The ability
to enhance specific domain performance while concurrently minimizing out-of-domain
degradation provides the ultimate empirical validation for the difficulty assignment

methodology.18

A fundamental requirement of a rigorous academic Master's thesis is that the intrinsic value of
the research remains absolute, irrespective of the final empirical outcomes. The
methodological design inherently generates epistemological value. The "Zero-Risk Academic
Framing" dictates exactly how the abstract, discussion, and conclusions must be positioned to
ensure the research is highly publishable regardless of whether the central hypothesis is
empirically validated, explicitly refuted, or yields null results.

1. Epistemological Value Independent of Empirical Triumph
The primary risk inherent in this thesis is that neither Strategy A (Input Masking) nor Strategy B
(Output Masking) significantly outperforms the standard Control baseline model. In a
traditional academic framing, this would be viewed as a failure of the hypothesis. However,
under the zero-risk framework, a null or negative result is aggressively framed as a critical
diagnostic discovery concerning the architectural limitations of sub-1-billion parameter models.
If both masking strategies fail to yield superior alignment, the thesis will pivot to an in-depth
analysis of representational fragility. The conclusion will argue that in highly compact
architectures (such as the 0.49B parameter Qwen-2.5), the internal attention mechanisms do
not distinctly decouple prompt context from sequential generation context. Therefore,
perturbing the input and perturbing the output degrade the conditional probability field
uniformly, rendering targeted token-masking an ineffective tool for difficulty-based curriculum
learning at this specific scale. This establishes a highly valuable, publishable boundary
condition: it proves that advanced masking-based curation techniques, which succeed robustly
in larger 7B+ models 3, possess a strict minimum parameter threshold to be effective.
Conversely, if one strategy severely degrades the model (e.g., Output Masking causes a
complete collapse in GSM8K mathematical performance), this negative result provides
profound insight into how small models learn logic. It would heavily suggest that 0.5B models
do not learn "reasoning" as a robust, internal sequence of logical steps, but rather rely entirely
on shallow heuristic mapping from the prompt context. The failure of the strategy, therefore,
perfectly illuminates the mechanistic reality of the lightweight model.

Regardless of the final benchmark scores, the development of the mathematical framework
itself constitutes a primary academic contribution. The formalization of

Furthermore, the extensive documentation of the single-GPU fine-tuning recipe, including the

severe hardware constraints. The thesis will position the comparative analysis not merely as a
contest between two filtering algorithms, but as a comprehensive, mechanistic autopsy of how
lightweight attention mechanisms respond to forced informational deficits during the critical
curation phase.
By meticulously decoupling the empirical success of the fine-tuned models from the analytical
success of the investigation, the thesis guarantees that its architectural insights, mathematical
definitions, and evaluations of parameter boundaries remain a permanent, valuable addition to
the literature on low-resource language model optimization.
